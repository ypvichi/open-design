A live-demo presenter view needs four panels

①
Current slide, large

Takes up over half the view, so you always know exactly what the audience is looking at.

②
Next slide preview

Lets you prep the transition line in advance instead of blanking on "what comes after this."

③
Live script panel

Large type, high contrast, scrollable — this is what the presenter is actually reading mid-demo.

④
Timer + slide count

How long you've talked and how many slides remain — the whole session's pacing runs on this.

A presenter view for a live AI demo needs four panels. I'll rank them by importance.

Panel one, the current slide, large. This has to take over half the view, because it's your sync anchor with the audience — whatever they're looking at has to be exactly what's in your head too.

Panel two, the next slide preview. A lot of people skip this one, so let me explain: the moment a live demo actually stalls isn't mid-sentence, it's the two seconds after you advance the slide. If you already know what's coming, the transition line writes itself.

Panel three, the live script — today's centerpiece, and the next part is entirely about it. One hard requirement up front: large type, high contrast, scrollable. You're only glancing at it out of the corner of your eye while the model generates on screen — small text means you can't read it in time.

Panel four, the timer and slide count. Knowing how long you've talked and how many slides remain is what keeps a live-generation demo from running over.

All four panels are non-negotiable, and this deck ships all four — press S and try it yourself.

// part 03 · pilot & risk control

Pilot format & 3 risk controls

01
Not a rehearsed script — a glance-and-go cue sheet

Bold the core point, isolate transition lines, list the numbers and names — so you can catch the thread with one glance while the model works.

02
150–300 words per slide, never more

Under 150 and the cue isn't enough; over 300 and there's no time to read it live. Pace each slide at 2–3 minutes.

03
Write it spoken, not written

"Therefore" becomes "so"; "the aforementioned approach" becomes "this approach." Read it aloud once — if it sounds stiff, rewrite it.

Now the part that determines whether the pilot is repeatable — how the live script gets written. Three rules.

First, the script is not a script. The moment you read verbatim from notes, the room can tell, and the "this is live" credibility disappears instantly.

Its real job is a glance-and-go cue sheet — bold the core points, isolate the transition lines, list the exact numbers and names. That way you catch the thread with one glance, but what comes out of your mouth is still your own words while the model generates on screen.

Second, keep each slide to 150–300 words. That's the range that survived a dozen live sessions. Under 150 and you stall mid-sentence; over 300 and you can't scan it in time. At 2–3 minutes per slide, that word count is exactly right.

Third, write it the way you'd actually say it. This is where most people trip up — write "therefore" and it comes out "so"; write "the aforementioned approach" and it comes out "this approach." Read it aloud once before the session; if it's stiff, rewrite it.

Put those three together and a live AI demo stops being a high-wire act.

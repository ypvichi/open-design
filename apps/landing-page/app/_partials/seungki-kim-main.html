Background

A KAIST designer who chose to make by hand.

Seungki spent eleven years at KAIST studying industrial design, through his PhD — then worked at Samsung Electronics, a media-art studio, and an AI startup. After all of it, he went back to the bench, founding FABOR.

For FABOR, 3D printing isn’t manufacturing — it’s a craft language: light, texture, pattern, structure. Layers of thin printed fabric are joined like Korean jogakbo (patchwork), with light and shadow moving through the structure.

But a studio of one is never only a maker. There’s a brand to build, a site to ship, a feed to keep alive — and every hour spent there is an hour away from the work. He needed a way to do both at once.

Why Open Design

Light to iterate. Still his to control.

Seungki came to Open Design from another AI design tool, and what made him stay was the freedom underneath it. He can choose the model he runs on — often a cheaper one — so a round of iteration costs little, and he can keep refining instead of settling for a first output.

It stays a tool, not a takeover. He wants an assistant that proposes options while he keeps the final call on every detail — control, not full automation. For Seungki, iteration matters more than one perfect first output.

Proof · the site

The physical work becomes a digital brand layer.

The physical work gives FABOR its language — and Open Design turns it into the brand around it. The fabor.kr micro-site, built mobile-first, and the card-news he posts to Instagram are all made in Open Design. He builds them in the seams of the real work: while a 3D print is running, he can ask Open Design to shape a micro-page, come back to review it, adjust the direction, and return to the bench. No separate production cycle, no days pulled away from the work.

Proof · the content

Card-news that carries his own voice.

The carousels hold FABOR’s voice as precisely as the objects do — the same technical, blueprint restraint, the same care in every layer. One tells the origin of the studio: KAIST, Samsung, an AI startup, then the turn to craft. Another walks through how a failed print left a single thin layer behind — and how that accident became his 3D-print fabric language, the moiré that now runs through the work. Both made in Open Design, on brand, ready to post.

The verdict
“Using Open Design allowed me to create branding content and micro-pages simultaneously while focusing on my hardware artwork.”

For a solo entrepreneur, this kind of parallel processing is crucial — and it has enabled me to dedicate more of my energy to the actual design of my pieces.

— Seungki Kim, Founder & Designer of FABOR
